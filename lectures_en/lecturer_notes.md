### Slide 8: Prologue: The Desire that Began the Digital Ecosystem
**Historical context: Evolution of communication**

**What to cover:**
- Transition: "Now let's understand HOW we got here"
- IoT didn't appear overnight—it's the culmination of 200 years of communication technology

**Timeline to present:**
- **1832:** Baron Schilling's electromagnetic telegraph (Russia)
  - First point-to-point electrical communication
  - Required human operators on both ends
  
- **1844:** Samuel Morse's public telegraph
  - Morse code standardized
  - First "digital" communication (dots and dashes)
  - Still required trained telegraphists

- **1876:** Alexander Bell's telephone
  - Voice communication over distance
  - Analog signal transmission
  - Human voice as the data carrier

- **1955:** Edward Thorp's wearable roulette computer
  - First wearable computer
  - Purpose: predict roulette outcomes
  - Shows early thinking about human-machine integration

- **1969:** ARPANET launch
  - Packet switching technology
  - Foundation of modern Internet
  - Military/academic origins

**Key message:** Each step removed human intermediaries from communication. IoT is the logical conclusion: machines talking directly.

### Slide 12: IoT System Architecture
**Three-layer hierarchical model**

**What to cover:**
- **Why architecture matters:**
  - IoT systems are complex
  - Need standardized way to understand them
  - IEEE established 3-layer model

- **The three layers:**

  **1. Perception Layer (sensors/actuators):**
  - The "senses" of the system
  - Touches the physical world
  - Converts physics to digital signals
  
  **2. Network Layer (gateways/routing):**
  - The "nervous system"
  - Transmits data from perception to application
  - Handles protocols, routing, connectivity
  
  **3. Application Layer (interfaces/analytics):**
  - The "brain"
  - Where data becomes knowledge
  - Dashboards, analytics, decision-making

- **Human analogy:**
  - Perception = skin, eyes, ears (sensing)
  - Network = spinal cord and nerves (transmission)
  - Application = brain (processing and decision)

- **Why layered architecture:**
  - Each layer can be updated independently
  - Replace a router without changing sensors
  - Upgrade analytics without touching network hardware
  - Modular design enables scalability

**Key message:** Layered architecture isolates complexity—change one layer without breaking others.

**Time allocation:** 4 minutes

### Slide 14: Perception Layer: Machine Senses
**Sensors and their characteristics**

**What to cover:**
- **What sensors do:**
  - Convert physical quantities to electrical signals
  - Voltage or current becomes digital number via ADC (Analog-to-Digital Converter)
  - This is the "translation" from physics to bits

- **Types of measurements:**
  - Temperature, humidity, light intensity
  - Gases (VOC, CO2, CO)
  - Pressure, motion (accelerometers)
  - Vibrations, geolocation (GPS)

- **Sensor classification:**

  **Passive sensors:**
  - Don't require power for detection
  - Example: photoresistor changes resistance with light
  - Simpler, lower power
  
  **Active sensors:**
  - Emit signal and measure reflection
  - Examples: ultrasound, LiDAR, radar
  - More complex, higher power, more precise

- **Calibration is critical:**
  - Sensors drift over time
  - Zero drift: reading changes even when measured quantity doesn't
  - Environmental factors affect accuracy
  - Regular calibration required for reliable data

- **The "hard risk":**
  - Physical environment destroys equipment
  - Extreme vibrations break solder joints
  - Moisture corrodes contacts
  - Temperature extremes damage components

**Key message:** Garbage in, garbage out—poor sensors make the entire IoT system worthless.

**Time allocation:** 4 minutes

**Practical tip:** Always budget for sensor calibration and replacement in IoT projects.

### Slide 15: Perception Layer: Actuators
**Executive elements that influence the physical world**

**What to cover:**
- **What are actuators:**
  - Devices that convert electrical signals back to physical action
  - The "muscles" of IoT systems
  - Without actuators, IoT can only observe, not change anything

- **Why feedback matters:**
  - Sensing is only half the solution
  - System must be able to REACT to what it senses
  - Example: roof that senses storm must have motors to close hatches

- **Types of actuators:**

  **Electric actuators:**
  - Semiconductor relays (switch high-power devices)
  - Servomechanisms (precise position control)
  - Stepper motors (controlled rotation)
  - Lighting (LEDs, smart bulbs)
  
  **Liquid/Gas actuators:**
  - Solenoid valves (control fluid flow)
  - Irrigation systems
  - Pressure relief valves
  - Pneumatic systems

- **The Control Loop:**
  1. Sensor measures (e.g., temperature drops)
  2. Decision made in higher layer (turn on heater)
  3. Actuator executes (relay closes, heater activates)
  4. Sensor confirms change (temperature rises)
  5. Loop continues

- **The "hard risk" warning:**
  - If sensor lies, actuator acts wrongly
  - Example: false frost reading → heaters turn on → fire hazard
  - This is why RTOS (Real-Time Operating Systems) need reliability guarantees

**Key message:** Actuators create real-world consequences—bad data can cause physical damage or danger.

**Time allocation:** 4 minutes

**Safety emphasis:** Always design fail-safes for actuator systems!

### Slide 16: Network Edge Gateway (Edge Gateway)
**Edge Computing and latency reduction**

**What to cover:**
- **What is a Gateway:**
  - Physical device (often DIN-rail mounted box)
  - Aggregates data from many sensors
  - Translates between protocols (e.g., Bluetooth → Ethernet)
  - First point of connection to "outside world"

- **Edge Computing paradigm:**
  - Old approach: send ALL data to cloud
  - Problem: bandwidth costs, latency, privacy
  - New approach: process at the edge, send only summaries
  
- **Example:**
  - Vibration sensor: 10,000 readings/second
  - Don't send all 10,000 values
  - Gateway calculates: average, max, min, frequency spectrum
  - Sends: "Bearing Status: Stable" once per minute
  - 99.99% data reduction!

- **Why Edge matters:**

  **Latency:**
  - Time for data to go: device → cloud → back with command
  - Can be hundreds of milliseconds to seconds
  - Critical applications can't wait:
    - Autonomous vehicle braking
    - Industrial emergency shutdown
    - Medical device response
  
  **Bandwidth:**
  - Transmitting raw data is expensive
  - Cellular data costs add up quickly
  - Edge filtering reduces costs

  **Reliability:**
  - What if Internet connection fails?
  - Edge gateway can still make local decisions
  - System doesn't completely fail

**Key message:** Edge Computing moves intelligence closer to where data is created—faster, cheaper, more reliable.

**Time allocation:** 4 minutes

**Real example:** Smart factory gateways process data from 100+ sensors but only send alerts to cloud.

### Slide 18: Hardware Platforms as the Cornerstone
**Arduino vs ESP32 vs Raspberry Pi comparison**

**What to cover:**
Point to the comparison image and walk through each platform:

**Arduino (Microcontroller):**
- Classic, educational platform
- Programs in C/C++ (Arduino IDE)
- NO built-in radio (needs add-on "shields")
- Simple, robust, huge community
- Best for: learning electronics, simple prototypes
- Power: low (tens of mA)

**ESP32 (System on Chip - SoC):**
- The "king" of professional IoT
- Built-in Wi-Fi AND Bluetooth
- Extremely low power in sleep mode (microamperes)
- Dual-core processor
- Best for: autonomous field sensors, commercial products
- Power: very low (sleep in μA, active ~100 mA)
- Price: $5-10

**Raspberry Pi (Single Board Computer):**
- Literally a full PC on a board
- Runs Linux (Raspberry Pi OS, Ubuntu)
- Much more powerful but power-hungry
- Gets hot under load (needs heatsink/fan)
- Best for: local MQTT broker, Edge gateway, home automation hub
- Power: high (several Watts continuous)
- Price: $35-80

**Key selection criteria:**
- Need Wi-Fi? → ESP32 or Pi
- Battery powered? → ESP32
- Need full OS? → Raspberry Pi
- Learning electronics? → Arduino

**Key message:** Choose the right tool—ESP32 dominates commercial IoT, Pi handles local servers, Arduino teaches fundamentals.

### Slide 19: "Deep Sleep" - the Power Compromise
**Power management in battery-powered devices**

**What to cover:**
- **The battery problem:**
  - Battery technology hasn't improved much in 40 years
  - Energy density increased only ~3x since 1980s
  - Meanwhile, electronics got vastly more efficient
  - Solution: aggressive power management, not bigger batteries

- **The Wi-Fi power problem:**
  - Wi-Fi module searching for network: 200-300 mA
  - Transmitting data: similar
  - Small battery (2000 mAh) would last hours, not years
  - Need different approach

- **Deep Sleep paradigm:**
  1. Device sleeps (almost zero power)
  2. RTC (Real-Time Clock) wakes it at scheduled time
  3. Processor wakes up (milliseconds)
  4. Read sensor (milliseconds)
  5. Transmit packet (milliseconds)
  6. Return to deep sleep
  7. Repeat

- **Power math:**
  - Active time: 1 second per hour
  - Sleep time: 3599 seconds per hour
  - Average current: microamperes
  - Result: 2-3 years on coin cell battery

- **Ultra Low Power (ULP) features:**
  - Main processor cuts its own power
  - Only RTC and minimal circuitry active
  - Some ESP32 variants have ULP co-processor

**Key message:** IoT batteries didn't get magical—engineers learned to turn things OFF.

**Time allocation:** 4 minutes

**Impressive stat:** Some LoRaWAN sensors run 10+ years on a single lithium battery!

### Slide 20: Digital Twins
**Virtual representations of physical systems**

**What to cover:**
- **What is a Digital Twin:**
  - Virtual copy of a physical object/system
  - Not just 3D visualization
  - Powered by REAL-TIME sensor data
  - 1:1 mapping between physical and digital

- **Why Digital Twins matter:**
  - Test changes virtually before implementing physically
  - No risk to actual equipment
  - Run "what if" simulations:
    - What if pump #3 fails during peak hours?
    - What if we increase conveyor speed by 15%?
    - What if fire starts in corner of hall?

- **Applications:**
  - Industrial machines (turbines, pumps, robots)
  - Buildings (HVAC systems, energy flows)
  - Cities (traffic patterns, utility networks)
  - Even ecosystems (forest carbon exchange models)

- **How it works:**
  - Physical sensors feed data to digital model
  - Model updates in real-time
  - Analysts interact with the twin, not the physical system
  - Changes tested in simulation first

- **Evolution:**
  - 1982: Check if Coca-Cola is cold (text output)
  - 2024: Millimeter-accurate 3D model with live sensor overlay
  - Next: AI-powered predictive simulations

**Key message:** Digital Twins let us fail safely in simulation before succeeding in reality.

**Time allocation:** 4 minutes

**Example:** NASA uses Digital Twins for spacecraft—test commands on Earth before sending to Mars.

### Slide 2: M2M Communication Problem
**The IoT Impossible Triangle**

**What to cover:**
- **The fundamental constraint:**
  - Show the triangle diagram
  - Three vertices: Range, Throughput, Battery Life
  - You can only have TWO of three
  - This is PHYSICS, not engineering limitation

- **Why this triangle exists:**
  - Long range requires more transmission power
  - High throughput requires more energy per second
  - Battery life requires minimizing both
  - Can't beat thermodynamics!

- **Examples of compromises:**

  **Range + Battery (LoRaWAN):**
  - Kilometers of range
  - Years on battery
  - Throughput: bytes per second
  - Use case: soil moisture sensor in field

  **Throughput + Range (Wi-Fi):**
  - Megabytes per second
  - Good range indoors
  - Battery: hours, not years
  - Use case: security camera (plugged in)

  **Throughput + Battery (BLE):**
  - Moderate data rate
  - Years on coin cell
  - Range: meters, not kilometers
  - Use case: fitness tracker

- **Ask the audience:**
  - "What would you choose for a smart door lock?"
  - "What about a wildlife tracking collar?"

**Key message:** Every IoT protocol is a compromise. Choose based on your priority.

### Slide 3: Wi-Fi (IEEE 802.11): Curse and Blessing
**Wi-Fi's role in IoT**

**What to cover:**
- **The paradox:**
  - Wi-Fi is EVERYWHERE
  - But it's TERRIBLE for most IoT applications
  - Why?

- **Advantages:**
  - Huge throughput (MB/s)
  - Every home has a router
  - Native IP protocol (talks directly to Internet)
  - No gateway needed

- **Disadvantages:**
  - Power-hungry architecture
  - Complex connection "handshake":
    1. Scan for networks
    2. Authenticate
    3. Obtain IP (DHCP)
    4. Establish TCP connection
    5. Finally send data (4 bytes of humidity!)
  -整个过程 takes seconds and hundreds of mA
  - Battery drains in days/weeks, not years

- **Where Wi-Fi IS appropriate:**
  - Constantly powered devices
  - Smart TVs, voice assistants
  - Security cameras

### Slide 4: Bluetooth and BLE (Low Energy) Revolution
**From Classic Bluetooth to BLE**

**What to cover:**
- **Historical context:**
  - Classic Bluetooth (1.0-3.0): designed for audio
  - Continuous stream (music, calls)
  - Radio always on
  - Battery: days at best

- **BLE revolution (Bluetooth 4.0+, 2011):**
  - Completely different architecture
  - Device sleeps most of the time
  - Wakes, sends packet in milliseconds, sleeps again
  - Current consumption: 100x lower than Classic

- **How BLE works:**
  - Advertising mode: "I'm here, here's my data"
  - Scan windows: listener opens briefly
  - Connection events: scheduled communication
  - Deep sleep between events

- **Applications:**
  - Fitness bands (Our Ring example from Lecture 1)
  - Smart watches
  - Medical devices (glucose monitors, pulse oximeters)
  - Location beacons (retail, museums)
  - Wireless peripherals (mice, keyboards)

- **Battery math:**
  - CR2032 coin cell: 220 mAh
  - BLE average current: 10-50 μA
  - Result: 1-3 years of operation

- **Range:**
  - Typically 10-30 meters indoors
  - BLE 5.0: up to 100+ meters (with lower data rate)

**Key message:** BLE sacrificed continuous connection for battery life—perfect for wearables.

### Slide 5: Zigbee and Z-Wave: Swarm Engineering (Mesh)
**Mesh network topology**

**What to cover:**
- **The range problem:**
  - Single radio: limited range
  - Walls, floors, interference reduce it further
  - How to cover a whole house/building?

- **Mesh network solution:**
  - Every device is also a router
  - Messages hop from device to device
  - Like a bucket brigade passing water
  - Range extends with each node

- **Zigbee specifics:**
  - Based on IEEE 802.15.4 standard
  - 2.4 GHz frequency (same as Wi-Fi)
  - Data rate: 20-250 kbps
  - Range per node: 10-20 meters
  - Mesh: unlimited hops (practically 5-10)

- **Device types in Zigbee:**
  - **Coordinator:** One per network (the hub)
  - **Router:** Mains-powered devices (repeat signals)
  - **End Device:** Battery sensors (sleep, can't route)

- **Self-healing:**
  - If one node fails, network finds new route
  - Automatic, no human intervention
  - Very robust

- **Z-Wave alternative:**
  - Sub-GHz frequency (868 MHz in EU, 908 MHz in US)
  - Better wall penetration (physics: lower frequency = better penetration)
  - Proprietary (not open standard)
  - Each manufacturer adds own layer (Philips Hue, IKEA TRÅDFRI)

- **The image:**
  - Show mesh diagram
  - Point out the failed node (red)
  - Show alternative route (dashed line)

**Key message:** Mesh networks trade complexity for coverage—every device extends the network.

### Slide 8: LoRaWAN vs NB-IoT: Private Network or Subscription?

### Slide 9: Sigfox: Ultra-Light Messages as a Service
**Network-as-a-Service model**

**What to cover:**
- **What is Sigfox:**
  - French company, global network
  - Ultra-narrowband technology
  - "Network as a Service" business model
  - Operator builds infrastructure, users pay per message

- **Extreme constraints:**
  - Maximum 140 messages per DAY per device
  - Payload: 12 BYTES per message
  - That's it!
  
- **What 12 bytes means:**
  - Can't send text strings
  - Can send: latitude (4 bytes) + longitude (4 bytes) + status (2 bytes) + battery (2 bytes)
  - Or: temperature (2 bytes) + humidity (2 bytes) + sensor ID (4 bytes) + checksum (2 bytes)
  - Extremely compact binary protocols required

- **Asymmetric architecture:**
  - Uplink (device → network): optimized
  - Downlink (network → device): very limited
  - Can't "query" device on demand
  - Device decides when to send

- **Use cases:**
  - Asset tracking (location once per hour)
  - Tank level monitoring (daily reading)
  - Alarm notifications (intrusion detected)
  - "I'm alive" heartbeat messages

- **NOT suitable for:**
  - Control systems (need downlink)
  - Rapid updates
  - Any application needing acknowledgment

- **Pricing:**
  - Very cheap per message
  - But limited messages
  - Business model: massive scale, tiny revenue per device

**Key message:** Sigfox is the "postcard" of IoT—one-way, tiny messages, extremely cheap, very limited.

### Slide 10: Why HTTP Protocol Doesn't Fit IoT?
**HTTP limitations for resource-constrained devices**

**What to cover:**
- **HTTP was designed for websites, not sensors:**
  - Web browsers have gigabytes of RAM
  - Microcontrollers have kilobytes
  - Mismatch creates problems

- **Problem 1: Header Overhead**
  - HTTP request example:
  ```
  GET /api/temperature HTTP/1.1
  Host: sensor.example.com
  User-Agent: ESP32/1.0
  Accept: application/json
  Content-Type: application/json
  Connection: keep-alive
  [body: {"temp": 22.5}]
  ```
  - Header: 150+ bytes
  - Data: 15 bytes
  - 90% overhead!

- **Problem 2: Request-Response Model**
  - HTTP is synchronous
  - Client sends request, must WAIT for response
  - Radio stays on during wait (power hungry)
  - What if server is slow or unreachable?
  - Timeout handling is complex

- **Problem 3: No Broadcast Support**
  - Each client must query server individually
  - 100 devices = 100 separate connections
  - Server must handle each independently
  - Doesn't scale

- **Visual comparison:**
  - HTTP packet: ~500 bytes total
  - MQTT packet: ~40 bytes total
  - 12x difference!

- **Memory constraints:**
  - ESP32 has ~520 KB RAM
  - Typical website: 2-5 MB
  - Won't fit!
  - Need lightweight protocols

**Key message:** HTTP is a Ferrari—great for highways, useless for dirt roads. IoT needs a bicycle.

**Time allocation:** 5 minutes

### Slide 11: MQTT: Lightweight, Binary IoT Standard
**Publish/Subscribe architecture**

**What to cover:**
- **What is MQTT:**
  - Message Queuing Telemetry Transport
  - Originally designed by IBM for oil pipelines (1999)
  - Now OASIS standard
  - THE protocol for IoT

- **Pub/Sub architecture:**
  - Traditional: client ↔ server (direct connection)
  - MQTT: client → broker ← client (mediated)
  
- **How it works:**
  1. Sensor connects to Broker
  2. Sensor PUBLISHES to TOPIC (e.g., "home/living/temp")
  3. Dashboard SUBSCRIBES to same topic
  4. Broker forwards message to dashboard
  5. Sensor and dashboard never know about each other!

- **Why this matters:**
  - Decoupling: devices don't need to know about each other
  - Scalability: add 100 subscribers, publisher doesn't care
  - Asynchronous: publisher doesn't wait for subscribers
  - Unreliable networks: broker buffers messages

- **The Broker:**
  - Central hub of MQTT system
  - Can be cloud-based (AWS IoT, Azure IoT Hub)
  - Or self-hosted (Mosquitto, EMQX)
  - Routes messages based on topics

- **Efficiency:**
  - Minimum packet size: 2 bytes header
  - Binary protocol (not text like HTTP)
  - No handshaking overhead after connection
  - Perfect for low-bandwidth links

**Key message:** MQTT's Pub/Sub model is the postal service of IoT—send to address, broker delivers to recipients.

**Time allocation:** 5 minutes

### Slide 12: MQTT: Topics, QoS, and "Last Will"
**Advanced MQTT features**

**What to cover:**
Three powerful features:

**1. Topics (hierarchical organization):**
- Format: `level1/level2/level3/...`
- Examples:
  - `home/living_room/temperature`
  - `factory/hall_B/conveyor_3/vibration`
  - `city/district_5/air_quality/pm25`
- Wildcards:
  - `+` single level: `home/+/temperature` (all rooms)
  - `#` multiple levels: `factory/#` (everything in factory)
- Best practice: environment separation
  - `dev/home/temp` (development)
  - `test/home/temp` (testing)
  - `prod/home/temp` (production)

**2. QoS (Quality of Service):**
- **QoS 0: At most once**
  - "Fire and forget"
  - No acknowledgment
  - Fastest, least reliable
  - Use case: frequent sensor data (missing one reading is OK)

- **QoS 1: At least once**
  - Publisher gets acknowledgment (PUBACK)
  - May receive duplicates
  - Good balance
  - Use case: important but not critical data

- **QoS 2: Exactly once**
  - Full handshake (4 messages)
  - Guaranteed delivery, no duplicates
  - Slowest, most overhead
  - Use case: billing data, critical commands

**3. Last Will and Testament (LWT):**
- Device sets "will message" when connecting
- If device disconnects unexpectedly, broker sends will
- Example: `home/alarm` → "Sensor offline!"
- Enables instant failure detection
- No need for polling "are you there?"

**Key message:** MQTT topics organize data, QoS controls reliability, LWT detects failures.

**Time allocation:** 5 minutes

### Slide 13: MQTT QoS Diagram
**Quality of Service sequence diagram**

**What to cover:**
Walk through the Mermaid diagram:

**QoS 0 (top section):**
- Single message: PUBLISH
- Sensor sends, broker receives
- No confirmation
- If message lost, it's gone
- Fastest: one transmission
- Use when: data is frequent, occasional loss acceptable

**QoS 1 (middle section):**
- Two messages: PUBLISH → PUBACK
- Sensor sends PUBLISH
- Broker responds with PUBACK (acknowledgment)
- If sensor doesn't get PUBACK, it resends
- Possible duplicate: broker might get same message twice
- Use when: need delivery confirmation, can handle duplicates

**QoS 2 (bottom section):**
- Four messages: PUBLISH → PUBREC → PUBREL → PUBCOMP
- Full handshake:
  1. PUBLISH: sensor sends data
  2. PUBREC: broker confirms receipt
  3. PUBREL: sensor releases (OK to finalize)
  4. PUBCOMP: broker completes (transaction done)
- Guaranteed exactly once
- Slowest but most reliable
- Use when: duplicate would cause problems (billing, critical commands)

**Trade-offs:**
- QoS 0: ~40 bytes, milliseconds
- QoS 2: ~200 bytes, seconds
- Higher QoS = more power consumption

**Key message:** Choose QoS based on consequence of lost/duplicate data.

**Time allocation:** 4 minutes

### Slide 14: CoAP and AMQP: Alternatives for Specific Scenarios
**Protocol alternatives**

**What to cover:**
Two alternatives to MQTT:

**CoAP (Constrained Application Protocol):**

- **Design philosophy:**
  - "HTTP for IoT"
  - Same concepts: GET, POST, PUT, DELETE
  - But lightweight implementation
  
- **Key difference: UDP instead of TCP**
  - UDP: connectionless, no handshake
  - Faster, less overhead
  - No delivery guarantee (fire and forget)
  - Can add reliability at application layer
  
- **Features:**
  - Built-in DTLS encryption (like TLS for UDP)
  - Supports multicast (send to many devices at once)
  - Very small code footprint
  
- **Use cases:**
  - Simple request-response interactions
  - Microcontrollers with minimal resources
  - When you need HTTP semantics without HTTP overhead

**AMQP (Advanced Message Queuing Protocol):**

- **Enterprise messaging:**
  - Designed for banking/finance
  - Complex message routing
  - Guaranteed delivery
  - Transaction support
  
- **Characteristics:**
  - Heavy protocol (large header)
  - Requires significant resources
  - Not suitable for microcontrollers
  - Used server-to-server
  
- **Implementation:**
  - RabbitMQ is popular AMQP broker
  - Cloud services support AMQP
  - Backend integration
  
- **When to use:**
  - Between cloud services
  - Enterprise message buses
  - When you need advanced routing/queuing

**Comparison:**
- MQTT: IoT standard, lightweight, Pub/Sub
- CoAP: HTTP-like, UDP, request-response
- AMQP: Enterprise, heavy, advanced features

**Key message:** MQTT is the IoT default; CoAP for simple HTTP-like needs; AMQP for enterprise backend.

### Slide 22: Good Security Practices (Defence in Depth)
**Multi-layer protection strategy**

**What to cover:**
- **Defence in Depth philosophy:**
  - Single security measure will fail
  - Multiple layers = multiple obstacles
  - Attacker must bypass ALL layers
  - Like castle: moat, walls, guards, keep

- **Four key practices:**

  **1. Identity (X.509 certificates):**
  - Each device has unique cryptographic identity
  - TLS/DTLS encryption
  - Mutual authentication (device ↔ server verify each other)
  - No more "admin/admin"
  
  **2. OTA Updates:**
  - Remote firmware updates
  - Patch vulnerabilities quickly
  - Cryptographic signing (verify update authenticity)
  - Automatic or scheduled
  - Rollback capability if update fails
  
  **3. Network Segmentation (VLAN):**
  - Separate IoT from main network
  - IoT VLAN: sensors, cameras, smart devices
  - Office VLAN: computers, servers, databases
  - Firewall between VLANs
  - If attacker breaks into IoT, can't reach sensitive data
  
  **4. Secure Boot:**
  - Verify code signature at startup
  - Only authorized firmware runs
  - Prevents malware persistence
  - Hardware root of trust

- **The diagram:**
  - Shows Office/Server VLAN (left)
  - IoT VLAN (right)
  - Firewall in between
  - IoT devices can't access network resources
  - Even if camera is compromised, database is safe

- **Additional best practices:**
  - Disable unused services/ports
  - Change all default credentials
  - Monitor network traffic for anomalies
  - Physical security (locked enclosures)
  - Regular security audits

**Key message:** Security isn't a feature—it's a process. Layer defenses, assume breaches will happen.

**Time allocation:** 5 minutes

### Slide 23: Privacy in the Era of Always-On Microphones
**Ethical considerations**

**What to cover:**
- **The privacy paradox:**
  - IoT collects intimate data
  - Often without explicit consent
  - Users don't understand implications
  - "Privacy is dead" vs. "Privacy by Design"

- **Smart Energy Meters:**
  - Measure consumption every 10 seconds
  - Seems innocent: billing accuracy
  - But reveals LIFE PATTERNS:
    - 07:30: kettle turns on (morning routine)
    - 08:30: power drops (left for work)
    - 14:00: power rises (returned home)
    - 23:00: lights off (bedtime)
  - **Who has this data?**
    - Energy supplier (legally)
    - Can be sold to third parties
    - Insurance companies might buy it
    - Divorce lawyers subpoena it
  
- **IoT Toys:**
  - "My Friend Cayla" doll (2014-2017)
  - Voice-activated, Internet-connected
  - Recorded children's conversations
  - Sent to cloud for processing
  - Unencrypted transmission
  - Stored on servers in foreign jurisdictions
  - **Result:** Banned in Germany, withdrawn from EU market
  - **Lesson:** Children's data requires highest protection

- **Fitness Bands:**
  - Track: pulse, activity, sleep, location
  - Health insurance implications:
    - Sedentary lifestyle detected
    - Premium increases automatically
    - No human decision, just algorithm
  - **Who owns this data?**
    - You? The manufacturer? The insurer?
  - **Can it be subpoenaed?**
    - Already happened in criminal cases
    - Fitness data as evidence

- **Privacy by Design:**
  - Build privacy into system from start
  - Data minimization (collect only what's needed)
  - Anonymization where possible
  - User consent (meaningful, not hidden)
  - Right to deletion
  - GDPR compliance (EU)

**Key message:** IoT data reveals intimate life details—engineers must design for privacy, not treat it as afterthought.
